Introduction to the Cave Lion

The Cave Lion (Panthera spelaea), also known as the European or Eurasian Cave Lion, roamed the cold landscapes of Europe and Asia during the Pleistocene epoch. It was one of the largest lion species to have ever lived and is often depicted in ancient cave paintings found across Europe. These majestic lions went extinct around 13,000 years ago, possibly due to climate change and hunting by early humans.

Habitat and Distribution

Cave Lions were found throughout northern Eurasia, inhabiting the steppes, forests, and tundras of regions that now include modern-day Europe, Russia, and parts of Asia. Their fossils have been discovered in many cave systems, leading to the species' name. They shared their habitat with other large Ice Age predators such as mammoths and saber-toothed cats.

Physical Characteristics

The Cave Lion was larger than most modern lions, with some males weighing up to 300 kg (660 lbs). Its build was muscular, with a powerful frame adapted for hunting large Ice Age prey.
